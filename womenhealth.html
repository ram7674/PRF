1. A Collaborative Project about Pre-Mature Deliveries with CDFD

Immune profiling in pre-term and term pregnancies: Reasons for premature deliveries. Profiling placental immune cell signatures to compare the physiological role of T-cell immune response in term births, pre-term births, and recurrent pregnancy loss.

Hospital based prospective cohort study. The primary outcome is period of gestation at birth to document preterm birth. Both vaginal and caesarean births would be analysed in this study.

bio-specimen type: Human placenta

Eligibility Criteria

Inclusion Criteria:

  • Placental samples from women who had a normal live, singleton vaginal/caesarean delivery.

Exclusion Criteria:

  • No associated congenital abnormalities
  • No associated intrauterine growth retardation / and
  • No associated co morbidity e.g
    • Eclampsia
    • Pre-eclampsia
    • Gestational diabetes
    • Chronic or acute infections
    • HIV
    • Any other associated malignancy

Brief Methodology

Isolation of T Lymphocytes from the Placenta:

  1. The collected tissue punch from placenta is to be stored in PBS till its processed.
  2. Thin slices of d. basalis are to be macroscopically dissected from the maternal-facing surface of the placenta. For early pregnancy only d. basalis connected to villous tissue to be used and processed similar to term placenta
  3. After dissection, the remains of villous tissue are to be cut off d. basalis with a scissor. D. parietalis was collected by removing the amnion and delicately scraping the d. parietalis from the chorion
  4. The obtained tissue to be washed thoroughly with firm shaking and thereafter finely minced between two scalpel blades in PBS. After mincing, decidual tissue has to be washed again until the supernatant is completely transparent to further minimize contamination with blood and villi.
  5. Decidual fragments are to be incubated with 0.2% collagenase IV and 0.02% DNAse I in RPMI-1640 medium, gently shaking in a water bath at 37â„ƒ for 60 min and thereafter washed once with RPMI.
  6. The resultant suspensions would be filtered through a 70 Âµm sieve, washed in RPMI, and layered on Ficoll Hypaque for density gradient centrifugation (20 min/800 g). Peripheral blood samples (if received) would be directly layered on a Ficoll Hypaque gradient.

2. Endometrial Regeneration in Infertility

A thin endometrium is the most common uterine infertility factor. In most cases, a thin endometrium is defined as â‰¤ 7 mm by ultrasound examination which varies between a range of â‰¤ 6 8 mm. The incidence of thin endometrium increases with age and is about 25% of women aged above 40 years of age in natural cycles. In in vitro fertilization (IVF) cycles, the percentage of thin endometrium is much lower, about 2.5%. However, these data are likely underestimated.

The treatment of thin endometrium is an ongoing challenge. Currently, there is no reliable, clinically approved therapeutic strategy, however, many approaches have been tried. They include hysteroscopic adhesiolysis, estrogen- and gonadotropin-releasing hormone agonist administration, vasoactive chemical measures (for example, aspirin, vitamin E, pentoxifylline, L-arginine, sildenafil), and intrauterine infusion of growth factor such as granulocyte-macrophage colony-stimulating factor, among others. These measures have no or little effect in most of the cases. Currently, the therapy with the use of cells and cell-based product seems to be the most promising approach, despite the lack of large-scale randomized clinical trials.

Prasad Research Foundation is actively involved in endometrial regeneration with the indigenous, innovative regenerative medicine product and ultimately aimed to translate it into clinical settings which can be effectively used in thin endometrium cases.
